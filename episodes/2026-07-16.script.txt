Top End (NT local).

An area about two-thirds the size of Tasmania has come under a landmark agreement with Traditional Owners in the Northern Territory. A large stretch of the Simpson Desert in the Territory's south-east is now protected through Aboriginal management, covering more than forty-seven thousand square kilometres. The Central Land Council says the deal will fund a co-ordinator and new ranger jobs, and will allow Traditional Owners to teach younger generations on country. Land custodians say it will help protect sacred sites, manage fire and control feral animals.

A cattle station seen as pivotal to the Northern Territory's cotton future is back on the market. Blackbull Station, more than fourteen thousand hectares in the Douglas-Daly region between Darwin and Katherine, combines irrigated farming, dryland cropping and grazing. It sits at the centre of the Territory's plan to grow one hundred thousand hectares of new broadacre crops by twenty-thirty, part of a wider effort to diversify an economy long built on cattle. In the last season, the Territory produced around forty-nine thousand bales of cotton.

Rec Fishing & the Bodies.

Recreational anglers in Victoria are being asked to have their say on a plan to improve the state's mulloway fishery. Developed with fishers themselves, the proposal would cut the daily limit from five fish to two, and lift the minimum size from sixty to seventy centimetres to give the species some ten months of extra protection. The fisheries authority says the changes are aimed at keeping the fishery healthy for the future, alongside new science and trial stocking. Mulloway, sometimes called jewfish, are a prized estuary and coastal target.

International & Destinations.

Off the New South Wales coast at Coffs Harbour, a new artificial reef has been lowered into the sea to boost fishing and tourism. It is made up of two steel structures, each weighing fifty tonnes and standing twelve metres tall, set about five hundred metres apart in twenty-eight metres of water. Engineers and biologists designed the reef to draw in mackerel, snapper, mulloway and baitfish, while standing up to a one-in-a-hundred-year storm. It joins a dozen other reefs already placed along the state's coastline.

Fly Fishing.

In Belize, fourteen young people from Caye Caulker have finished their island's first ever fly-fishing camp. Led by a San Pedro flats guide known as the Doctor of the Flats, the five-day program mixed casting instruction with lessons in sustainable fishing and marine conservation, before finishing with a day on the saltwater flats. The guide says he hopes some of these children will become the next generation of flats fishing guides. It is exactly the kind of grounding in saltwater fly fishing that builds a lifelong respect for the water.

Social & Catches.

A family fishing trip on the Minnesota River turned into something to remember when seven-year-old Isaac Richert, still in his pyjamas, hauled in a gar measuring thirty and a half inches. Fishing with his father and older brother, the youngster set a new Minnesota state record with the prehistoric-looking catch. It adds to what has been a remarkable year of record fish in the state. Proof that you are never too young, or too casually dressed, to land the fish of a lifetime.

In Idaho, a young angler has made her mark on the state record books with an unusual catch. While fishing the Salmon River, Akeley Fahnholz landed a chiselmouth, a native fish from the minnow family that scrapes algae off river rocks with a specially shaped jaw and rarely grows beyond about a foot long. Because such records are set for fish released alive, she measured, photographed and returned it to the water. Idaho Fish and Game has now certified it as the new state catch-and-release record for the species.

Lighter Side.

Off the coast of Maine, two experienced lobster fishermen have each pulled up one of the rarest catches in their trade, and they did it just days apart. Both landed a split-coloured lobster, an oddity thought to occur in only about one in fifty million, caused by a genetic quirk that scientists still do not fully understand. One captain, who made his catch on Georges Bank, plans to donate the lobster to a science centre so others can see it. Two such catches in one week is astonishing luck.

Conservation.

A major clean-up in the Torres Strait has cleared about forty-nine tonnes of harmful marine debris from northern Australian waters. Working together, the Australian Fisheries Management Authority and Parks Australia removed three abandoned foreign fishing vessels and their gear from three locations. One of the vessels had beached at Crab Island, a vital nesting site for the flatback turtle, where it was dismantled and the beach cleaned up. The others, near Horn Island and Pearce Cay, were retrieved and safely disposed of.

There is heartening news for one of Australia's most distinctive small mammals. The numbat, a striped, termite-eating marsupial, has been officially moved off the endangered list after some forty years of conservation effort. Careful predator control and reintroductions have gradually lifted its numbers. Conservationists are quick to add that the work is far from over, with introduced predators still a serious threat, but for now it stands as a genuine success story.
