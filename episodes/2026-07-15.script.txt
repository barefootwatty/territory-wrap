Barefoot Daily News Bulletin — Wednesday 15 July 2026.

Good morning and welcome to the Barefoot Daily News Bulletin for Wednesday the fifteenth of July, twenty twenty-six. Here is the good news from the water, the outdoors and the Top End.

In today's bulletin: Forty-nine tonnes of debris cleared from Torres Strait waters; New artificial reef installed off Coffs Harbour; More than 200 'cod hotels' to rebuild fish habitat in South East Queensland; Wellington Point angler shares a lifelong love of Moreton Bay; A quiet reflection on protecting the wild waters we fish; Gone Fishing Weekend packages open for clubs in October.

Rec Fishing & the Bodies.

A major clean-up has cleared roughly forty-nine tonnes of harmful marine debris from waters in the Torres Strait. Working across autumn, the Australian Fisheries Management Authority and Parks Australia recovered three abandoned foreign fishing vessels and their gear from Crab Island, Horn Island and Pearce Cay. At Crab Island, an important flatback turtle nesting site, a beached vessel was dismantled and its steel removed, while an eighty-metre net was hauled from the wreck at Pearce Cay and taken ashore for disposal.

A new artificial reef has been placed off Coffs Harbour to boost coastal tourism and sustainable recreational fishing. It is made up of two steel structures, each weighing fifty tonnes and standing twelve metres tall, set about five hundred metres apart in twenty-eight metres of water and built to survive a one-in-one-hundred-year storm. Designed to draw in mackerel, snapper and mulloway, it becomes the thirteenth such reef along the New South Wales coast.

Fly Fishing.

In a thoughtful piece for Hatch Magazine, writer Johnny Carrol Sain remembers a hidden creek that could once be reached only by map, compass, or a trusted word, and considers how quickly crowds can wear away the wild feel of such places. His argument is a simple one: unspoiled water holds a worth beyond profit, and that magic is easily lost. It is a gentle reminder, from one angler to another, of why quiet waters are worth protecting.

Social & Catches.

A Wellington Point angler, AJ Amos-Maclean, has shared a lifelong love of fishing Moreton Bay, together with a keen interest in marine conservation. Among her recent rewards was a good-sized snapper, one of the species the bay is well known for. It is a reminder of the simple pleasure that keeps recreational anglers coming back to the water.

Lighter Side.

Something to look forward to for the clubs: packages are now available to help fishing clubs and organisations host their own events for Gone Fishing Weekend. The weekend lands on Saturday the seventeenth and Sunday the eighteenth of October, and the idea is simply to get more people out enjoying recreational fishing together.

Conservation.

More than two hundred purpose-built shelters known as cod hotels are being placed in South East Queensland waterways to rebuild habitat for native fish, among them the critically endangered Mary River cod. Made from recycled hollow logs and natural timber, they copy the sheltered spots fish rely on to breed, feed and hide, with the first now going into the Mid and Upper Brisbane River in the Somerset region. The effort is part of the Resilient Rivers program and sits alongside other work, including a new fishway at Gould Adams Park.
